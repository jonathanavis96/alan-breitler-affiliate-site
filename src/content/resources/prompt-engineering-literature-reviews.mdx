## Introduction

A well-crafted prompt can mean the difference between a wall of irrelevant citations and a focused, high-quality reading list. In this guide, we walk through practical prompt-engineering strategies tailored for academic literature reviews.

## Why Prompt Design Matters for Research

Most AI research assistants rely on natural-language queries to search, rank, and summarise papers. Generic queries return generic results. By structuring your prompts with clear scope, methodology hints, and output-format instructions, you can dramatically improve relevance.

## Core Techniques

### 1. Scope-Framing Prompts

Start by telling the AI **what** you are reviewing and **why**. Include your discipline, the time range of interest, and any methodological filters.

> *Example:* "Find empirical studies published between 2020 and 2025 on transformer-based models applied to biomedical named-entity recognition. Prioritise papers with benchmark comparisons."

### 2. Synthesis Prompts

After gathering sources, ask the AI to **compare and contrast** findings rather than simply listing them.

> *Example:* "Summarise the key methodological differences between the top five papers on zero-shot classification in clinical NLP, focusing on dataset size, evaluation metrics, and reported F1 scores."

### 3. Gap-Identification Prompts

Use prompts that explicitly request the AI to highlight **what is missing** in the literature.

> *Example:* "Based on these ten abstracts, identify research gaps and under-explored sub-topics related to low-resource language adaptation of large language models."

## Tips for Better Results

- **Be specific about output format** — ask for tables, bullet lists, or structured summaries.
- **Iterate** — refine prompts based on initial outputs; treat it as a conversation.
- **Cross-validate** — always verify AI-surfaced citations against primary databases like PubMed or Semantic Scholar.
- **Set constraints** — specify journal tiers, citation counts, or date ranges to narrow results.

## Conclusion

Prompt engineering is not a one-size-fits-all skill — it improves with practice and domain knowledge. Start with the techniques above, adapt them to your field, and you will find that AI research assistants become genuinely useful collaborators in your literature review process.
