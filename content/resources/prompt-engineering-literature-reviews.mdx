A well-crafted prompt can mean the difference between a wall of irrelevant citations and a focused, high-quality reading list. In this guide, we walk through practical prompt-engineering strategies tailored for academic literature reviews — whether you are writing a thesis chapter, a systematic review, or simply trying to map an unfamiliar field.

## Step 1: Define Your Research Scope

Before opening any AI tool, crystallise what you are looking for. Write down:

- **Your research question** — be as specific as possible.
- **Discipline boundaries** — are you staying within one field or crossing domains?
- **Time range** — recent work only, or a historical sweep?
- **Methodology filters** — empirical, theoretical, meta-analysis?

> *Example prompt:* "Find empirical studies published between 2020 and 2025 on transformer-based models applied to biomedical named-entity recognition. Prioritise papers with benchmark comparisons."

## Step 2: Craft Scope-Framing Prompts

Tell the AI **what** you are reviewing and **why**. Include your discipline, the time range of interest, and any methodological filters. Scope-framing prompts reduce noise dramatically.

- Start with a role instruction: *"Act as a research librarian specialising in computational linguistics."*
- Specify output format: tables, bullet lists, or annotated bibliographies.
- Set explicit constraints: journal tiers, citation counts, or date ranges.

## Step 3: Use Synthesis Prompts to Compare Sources

After gathering an initial set of papers, ask the AI to **compare and contrast** findings rather than simply listing them.

> *Example:* "Summarise the key methodological differences between the top five papers on zero-shot classification in clinical NLP, focusing on dataset size, evaluation metrics, and reported F1 scores."

Synthesis prompts transform a flat list into a structured understanding of the landscape.

## Step 4: Identify Gaps with Targeted Prompts

Use prompts that explicitly request the AI to highlight **what is missing** in the literature.

> *Example:* "Based on these ten abstracts, identify research gaps and under-explored sub-topics related to low-resource language adaptation of large language models."

Gap-identification prompts help you position your own contribution and frame your literature review's narrative arc.

## Step 5: Iterate and Cross-Validate

No single prompt yields a perfect literature review. Treat the process as a conversation:

1. **Run your initial prompt** and review the first batch of results.
2. **Refine** — narrow or broaden based on what you see.
3. **Cross-validate** — always verify AI-surfaced citations against primary databases like PubMed, Semantic Scholar, or Google Scholar.
4. **Export and organise** — move verified references into your reference manager (Zotero, Mendeley, etc.).

## Tips for Better Results

- **Be specific about output format** — ask for tables, bullet lists, or structured summaries.
- **Iterate** — refine prompts based on initial outputs; treat it as a conversation.
- **Cross-validate** — always verify AI-surfaced citations against primary databases.
- **Set constraints** — specify journal tiers, citation counts, or date ranges to narrow results.
- **Combine tools** — use Consensus for paper discovery and Elicit for extraction and synthesis.
